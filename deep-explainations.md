## Station 1 — Light Laboratory

### Puzzle 1: Additive Colour Mixing (RGB)

Most people learn to mix colour with paint, where combining everything eventually makes mud. Light works the opposite way: mixing coloured light together produces **lighter** results, not darker ones. This is called **additive mixing**.

The three primary colours of light are **red**, **green**, and **blue** (RGB). When all three are combined at full intensity they produce white light. This is why screens, projectors, and stage lighting all use RGB — they are emitting light, not reflecting it.

The reason these three are chosen is biological: the human eye contains three types of cone cells, each most sensitive to red, green, or blue wavelengths. Every colour you see on a screen is your brain interpreting a blend of signals from those three cone types. A vivid yellow on your monitor contains no yellow light at all — it is a carefully balanced red and green signal that your eye reads as yellow.

Partial overlaps produce secondary colours. Red + Green = Yellow. Green + Blue = Cyan. Red + Blue = Magenta. This is why the RGB secondaries are exactly the CMY primaries — the two systems are mirrors of each other.

**Key terms**
- **Additive mixing**: combining light sources so their wavelengths sum together
- **Primary colours of light**: red, green, blue — cannot be created by mixing other light
- **Luminance**: the overall brightness of a colour; adding more light always increases it

### Puzzle 2: Subtractive Colour Mixing (CMY)

When you paint on paper or print an image, colour works by **subtraction**. A cyan pigment looks cyan because it absorbs red wavelengths from white light and reflects everything else back to your eye. It is removing (subtracting) part of the spectrum, not adding to it.

The three subtractive primaries are **cyan**, **magenta**, and **yellow** (CMY). These are the inks used in colour printing for this reason — each one absorbs a third of the visible spectrum. Overlap two of them and you absorb two-thirds: Cyan + Magenta absorbs red and green, reflecting only blue. Cyan + Yellow absorbs red and blue, reflecting green. Magenta + Yellow absorbs blue and green, reflecting red.

A critical observation from printing: mixing all three subtractive primaries in theory should produce black, since all wavelengths are absorbed. In practice it produces a murky dark brown, because pigments are chemically impure. This is why printing presses add a separate black (K) ink — hence CMYK. The K stands for "key" (the key printing plate used in traditional lithography, which was always black).

Understanding this distinction between additive and subtractive mixing is foundational. Confusion between the two is why beginners sometimes try to use colour wheel theory for screen design, or apply RGB logic to paint, and get unexpected results.

**Key terms**
- **Subtractive mixing**: combining pigments or filters that each absorb part of the spectrum
- **Primary colours of pigment**: cyan, magenta, yellow — each absorbs one-third of white light
- **CMYK**: the four-ink print system that adds black to compensate for impure pigment

### Puzzle 3: Chromatic Black and Luminous Shadows

Mixing a neutral black with pigments seems straightforward — just use black paint. But experienced painters often avoid tube black entirely, because it kills the life in a colour. A shadow mixed with pure black tends to look flat, dead, and disconnected from the rest of the painting.

Instead, painters mix **chromatic blacks** using complementary pigments: colours that sit opposite each other on the colour wheel. Blue + Orange, Red + Green, Yellow + Purple — any complementary pair, when mixed in the right ratio, neutralises toward a very dark, near-neutral colour that retains a subtle, living hue. The resulting dark has an inner glow that tube black cannot achieve.

This also applies to painting shadows. Real shadows are not neutral grey — they carry colour information from the surrounding environment. A shadow on warm skin in afternoon light is often cooler and slightly violet. A shadow on a white wall near a green garden may carry a green cast. Treating shadows as simply "darker versions of the surface colour" produces more convincing results than mixing in black.

The concept that a dark area can still hold meaningful hue is sometimes called **chromatic depth**. Rembrandt and the Old Masters used this extensively — their darkest passages still vibrate with subdued colour.

**Key terms**
- **Chromatic black**: a near-neutral dark mixed from complementary pigments rather than tube black
- **Complement**: the colour directly opposite on the colour wheel
- **Chromatic depth**: shadows and darks that retain a subtle, living hue rather than going flat grey

## Station 2 — Value Sketchboard

### Puzzle 4: Value Structure and the Squint Test

**Value** is the lightness or darkness of a colour, completely independent of its hue. A bright red and a bright green can have identical value. A dark navy and a dark burgundy can also have identical value. Value is the greyscale skeleton beneath all colour.

Value structure is arguably the most important single element in a painting. Colour can be wrong; value cannot be. If you photograph a painting in black and white and it reads clearly — if you can tell what is foreground, midground, and background, and where the light is coming from — the painting has strong value structure. If it turns into an illegible grey fog, the colour is doing all the work and the value is failing.

The **squint test** is one of the oldest tricks in figurative painting. Squinting reduces your eye's ability to resolve fine detail and colour nuance, collapsing everything into simplified masses of light and dark. It forces you to see value relationships rather than surface texture or hue. If a composition holds up under a squint — if the main shapes remain readable — the value structure is sound. If it falls apart, the values are too close together.

This is also why artists talk about grouping values: keeping your lights together and your darks together rather than scattering high-contrast patches everywhere. Scattered contrast is visually noisy. Massed values are calm and readable.

**Key terms**
- **Value**: the lightness or darkness of a colour on a scale from black to white
- **Value structure**: the overall arrangement of light and dark masses in a composition
- **Local colour**: the actual colour of a surface as seen in neutral light, before shadows or highlights modify it

### Puzzle 5: The Value Scale

A **value scale** (or value ladder) is a sequence of tones arranged in even steps from pure black to pure white. Constructing and memorising a value scale trains your eye to judge relative lightness accurately — a skill that underlies all strong colour mixing and painting decisions.

Most practical value scales divide the range into 9 or 10 steps. The key skill is **interval accuracy**: each step should feel equally far from its neighbours, not bunched at one end. Beginners tend to bunch values in the midtone range, avoiding true darks and true lights. A well-trained eye spreads the full range.

Value scales also teach an important perceptual fact: context changes perceived value. A mid-grey square on a white background looks darker than the same grey square on a black background. This is **value relativity** — there is no such thing as an "absolute" light or dark, only light or dark *relative to what surrounds it*. Painters exploit this constantly, making an object appear to glow simply by darkening what surrounds it.

Value organisation also underpins compositional hierarchy. The area of highest value contrast in a painting draws the eye first. Placing your lightest light next to your darkest dark at the focal point is one of the most reliable ways to control viewer attention.

**Key terms**
- **Value scale**: a graded sequence of tones from darkest to lightest
- **High key**: a painting or palette dominated by light values
- **Low key**: a painting or palette dominated by dark values
- **Tonal range**: the span from the darkest to lightest value used in a composition

#### The Three Dimensions of Colour

In visual fundamentals, colour is understood through a triadic system:

1. **Hue** - the identity of the wavelength
1. **Value** - the lightness or darkness
1. **Chroma** - the intensity or purity of the colour

While beginners often rely solely on hue, the master artist uses Chroma to establish focal points, create compositional hierarchy, and shape emotional resonance.

Chroma - distinct from the more casual term saturation—describes the vividness or purity of a colour relative to the physical limits of that hue's gamut.
A "fire-engine red" sits near maximum chroma; a "dusty rose" has been pulled toward the neutral centre of colour space.

Chroma States

## Chroma States

| **State**       | **Characteristics**                                   | **Additive / Mixing Method**                                      |
|-----------------|--------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **High Chroma** | Vivid, pure, intense; maximum visual impact           | Pure pigment; minimal mixing; transparent glazing                  |
| **Low Chroma**  | Muted, neutralised, dusty; recedes to provide rest    | Mixing with white, grey, black, or complements                     |



Understanding a pigment's baseline purity is essential. Once you know a hue's maximum intensity, you can avoid accidental mud and instead make deliberate, controlled adjustments.

#### The Mechanics of Desaturation

A common challenge for students is managing the hierarchy of attention.

If everything is high-chroma, nothing stands out. The eye becomes fatigued.

To create a sophisticated palette, you must master desaturation-the intentional reduction of chroma.

Four Neutral Additives

- **White** - creates a tint; raises value and reduces chroma
- **Grey** - creates a tone; neutralises without major value shift
- **Black** - creates a shade; darkens and suppresses chroma
- **Complements** - neutralise while maintaining harmonic coherence

> **Critical Application**: The Professional's Method
> Mixing with complements is the most elegant and natural way to reduce chroma.
> It preserves the logic of the palette and avoids the "deadness" of black-mixed shadows.

Even so, chromatic behaviour is ultimately governed by the physical and perceptual limits of human vision, not by digital sliders.

#### The Munsell Relationship: Chroma and Value

Chroma is inseparable from Value.

Albert Munsell's early 20th-century system mapped this relationship and revealed a crucial truth:

Different hues reach their peak chroma at different value levels.

Because human eyes are not equally sensitive to all wavelengths, the colour space is not a neat geometric solid-it is an irregular, "spiky" structure.

Hierarchy of Peak Chroma

- **Yellow** - most vivid at high values (near white)
- **Orange** - peaks at mid-high values
- **Violet** - most vivid at low values (near black)

> Critical Application: Avoiding the Digital Trap Digital HSB sliders create a false equivalence. A violet at "100% brightness" is not perceptually equal to a yellow at "100% brightness."
> If you lighten violet toward white, you must lose chroma.
> This is not a flaw-it is a physical limitation of both pigment and perception.

The Munsell "colour tree" remains irregular because it reflects reality, not convenience.

#### Summary for the Student Artist

Mastering chroma is the bridge between beginner colour use and true artistic control.

**Critical Takeaways**

1. **Chroma vs Saturation**:
	- Saturation = distance from neutral grey.
	- Chroma = purity of the hue across value levels.
1. **Desaturation Hierarchy**:
	- Complements create the most natural, integrated neutrals.
1. **Hues Are Not Equal**:
	- Each hue peaks at a specific value.
	- Forcing a hue away from its natural peak always reduces chroma.
1. **The Digital Trap**:
	- HSB sliders lie.
	- Trust perceptual models (like Munsell), not software abstractions.

**Key terms**
- **Chroma / Saturation**: the purity or intensity of a colour; how far it is from neutral grey
- **Peak chroma**: the value level at which a given hue reaches its maximum possible saturation
- **Munsell system**: a colour notation system that models hue, value, and chroma as three independent dimensions

## Station 3 — Colour Wheel Table

### Puzzle 7: Complementary Colours

**Complementary colours** sit directly opposite each other on the colour wheel: red and green, blue and orange, yellow and violet. Their relationship is one of the most powerful and most misused tools in colour theory.

When placed side by side at full saturation, complementaries **intensify each other**. An orange looks more orange next to blue. A red looks more red next to green. This is not an optical illusion so much as a perceptual law — the eye's opponent colour channels make each hue activate a response that heightens its opposite. This is why sports teams and advertising designers so often use complementary pairs: they demand attention.

When **mixed together** as pigments, the same complementaries neutralise each other, moving toward grey or brown depending on the pigments used. This makes them extremely useful for desaturating a colour without going to black. Instead of muddying a too-bright green with black, a painter adds a small amount of red to knock the saturation back in a more naturalistic way.

A common mistake is treating all "opposite" hues as equal complements. The exact position on the wheel matters. A red-orange's true complement is blue-green, not pure blue. Identifying true complements requires either a colour wheel reference or a trained eye.

**Key terms**
- **Complementary colours**: hues directly opposite each other on the colour wheel
- **Simultaneous contrast**: the visual intensification that occurs when complements are placed adjacent
- **Neutralising**: reducing chroma by mixing a colour with its complement

### Puzzle 8: Triadic Harmony

A **triadic colour scheme** uses three hues spaced equally around the colour wheel, approximately 120° apart. The classic triad is red, yellow, and blue — the traditional primaries. But the scheme works at any rotation: red-orange, yellow-green, and blue-violet form a triad, as do orange, green, and violet.

Triadic schemes are inherently energetic. All three hues contrast with each other, so there is no natural dominant — the eye is pulled in three directions simultaneously. This can be powerful or exhausting depending on how the palette is handled. The key to a working triadic palette is **unequal distribution**: one hue should dominate the composition, a second should support, and the third should appear sparingly as an accent. When all three compete equally, the result is visual noise.

Triads also benefit from value and saturation variation. Using all three hues at full saturation amplifies the tension. Desaturating two of them and allowing the accent colour to carry full chroma immediately creates hierarchy and calm.

Understanding triadic harmony helps explain why some three-colour combinations feel balanced and exciting while others feel chaotic: it is not just which colours are used but *how much* of each is present.

**Key terms**
- **Triadic harmony**: a colour scheme using three hues spaced 120° apart on the colour wheel
- **Colour hierarchy**: the deliberate assignment of dominant, secondary, and accent roles to hues in a composition
- **Hue rotation**: the position of a colour measured in degrees around the colour wheel (0°–360°)

### Puzzle 9: Colour and Emotional Tone

Colour combinations communicate mood before the viewer reads a single word or consciously processes any image. This is partly cultural convention, partly hardwired response to light and temperature.

**Warm hues** — reds, oranges, yellows — activate and energise. They are associated with fire, sunlight, and physical warmth, and they tend to advance toward the viewer optically. **Cool hues** — blues, blue-greens, violets — recede and calm. They are associated with sky, water, and shadow.

But hue temperature alone does not determine mood. **Value** and **saturation** contribute equally. High saturation + high value contrast = excitement and urgency (think carnival posters). Low saturation + close values = introspection and calm (think mist, early morning fog). Dark values + desaturated hues = dread or solemnity (think Gothic interiors, horror film palettes). Understanding this means a "calm" palette is not simply blue — it requires low-contrast values, moderate saturation, and likely cool hues working together.

A further nuance: creative subversion of expectations carries meaning too. A horror film that uses pastel pinks and yellows is disturbing *because* those colours are "supposed" to be safe. The mismatch between expectation and context creates unease. Competent colour storytelling requires knowing the convention in order to break it intentionally.

**Key terms**
- **Colour temperature**: the warm (orange-red) to cool (blue-violet) axis of the colour wheel
- **Simultaneous contrast**: how surrounding colours alter the perceived temperature and mood of a focal colour
- **Chromatic key**: the overall emotional register of a palette, determined by hue, value, and saturation together

## Station 4 — Optical Illusion Wall

### Puzzle 10: Simultaneous Contrast

**Simultaneous contrast** is the phenomenon where a colour's appearance changes depending on the colours that surround it. Two physically identical grey squares can look like different shades of grey if one is surrounded by dark and the other by light. The same mid-blue can look cool and retreating against orange, or warm and dominant against violet.

This was documented extensively by the French chemist Michel-Eugène Chevreul in the 1830s, working at the Gobelins tapestry factory, where he noticed that the same thread colour looked different depending on which threads surrounded it. His findings — later expanded by Josef Albers in his book *Interaction of Color* — remain foundational to colour theory and design.

The practical implications are significant. A colour that looks perfect on your palette or on a colour swatch may look wrong in the painting, because it is now surrounded by other colours that shift its apparent hue, value, and saturation. This is why artists assess colours in context, not in isolation. It also explains why interior designers place paint chips against a large area of the existing room colour rather than looking at the chip alone.

Chevreul's law states that adjacent colours always push each other toward their mutual complement. A warm colour makes its neighbour look cooler. A dark surround makes a centre colour look lighter.

**Key terms**
- **Simultaneous contrast**: the perceptual shift in a colour's appearance caused by adjacent colours
- **Chromatic induction**: a colour's hue being influenced (induced) by the hue of a surrounding area
- **Albers' Interaction of Color**: landmark 1963 study demonstrating how extensively context modifies perceived colour

### Puzzle 11: Induced Colour (Making Grey Look Blue)

A neutral grey has no hue of its own — it is equally balanced across all wavelengths. Because of this, it is particularly susceptible to **chromatic induction**: it will take on the appearance of the complementary colour of whatever surrounds it.

Surround a grey square with orange, and the grey will appear slightly blue-violet — orange's complement. Surround it with green, and the same grey will appear slightly reddish. The grey has not changed; only its context has changed.

This effect was used deliberately by the Impressionists and Post-Impressionists. Claude Monet often painted shadows with cool blues and violets not because shadows *are* that colour, but because the warm-lit surrounding areas induced that complementary cool shift in the viewer's eye. Paul Cézanne's grey stones appear different colours depending on which passage of the painting you look at before resting on them.

The strength of the induction depends on the **saturation** and **size** of the surrounding area. A small ring of low-saturation orange will barely shift a grey. A large, vivid orange field can push the same grey toward an unmistakable blue-violet. Painters and cinematographers use this to control perceived colour temperature without directly changing the neutral element itself.

**Key terms**
- **Chromatic induction**: a neutral colour taking on the complementary hue of its surroundings
- **Colour context**: the surrounding hues, values, and saturations that modify how a focal colour is perceived
- **Afterimage**: the related phenomenon where staring at a colour and then looking at grey induces its complement

### Puzzle 12: The Role of Neutrals and Saturation Hierarchy

When every element in a composition is highly saturated, nothing stands out. The viewer's eye has nowhere to rest and no clear focal point. This is the visual equivalent of a room where everyone is shouting at once.

**Neutrals** — greys, browns, muted earth tones — are the silence that gives the accent colours somewhere to live. A single vivid orange surrounded by muted grey-browns reads with ten times the visual punch of that same orange surrounded by other saturated colours. The neutrals do not "do nothing" — they are actively creating the conditions for the accent to succeed.

This is sometimes framed as the **figure-ground relationship** applied to colour: the neutral is the ground, the saturated accent is the figure. The eye is drawn first to areas of maximum saturation contrast, just as it is drawn to areas of maximum value contrast.

Mixing naturalistic neutrals is itself a skill. Rather than using tube grey or tube brown, skilled painters mix neutrals from complements or from near-complementary pairs. These **chromatic neutrals** carry a subtle hue and feel integrated into the painting's overall colour key, unlike the flat deadness of a neutral from a tube.

**Key terms**
- **Saturation hierarchy**: the deliberate distribution of colour intensity from most neutral (background) to most vivid (focal point)
- **Chromatic neutral**: a grey or brown mixed from complements rather than from black and white, retaining a subtle hue cast
- **Focal accent**: a small area of high saturation used to direct the viewer's attention

## Station 5 — Window Landscape

### Puzzle 13: Atmospheric Perspective

**Atmospheric perspective** (also called aerial perspective) describes the way distance modifies how we see colour and edge. The further away an object is, the more air lies between it and the observer — and air is not invisible. It scatters light, particularly at the blue end of the spectrum, and this accumulation of scattered light has predictable visual effects.

Four things happen to a colour as it recedes into distance:

1. **Saturation decreases** — vivid colours become muted and grey
2. **Value rises toward the sky tone** — darks lighten and lights also lighten, compressing the value range
3. **Colour temperature cools** — hues shift toward blue and blue-grey
4. **Edge contrast softens** — sharp edges become diffuse and lost

Leonardo da Vinci wrote about aerial perspective in his notes, and it became a standard technique in landscape painting from the Renaissance onwards. Turner used it with particular mastery, building up layers of thin, cool glazes to push background mountains far into the distance.

Ignoring atmospheric perspective produces paintings where distant mountains look as crisp and saturated as foreground trees — a flat, unconvincing result. Applying it even subtly creates a convincing illusion of deep space.

**Key terms**
- **Atmospheric perspective**: the visible effect of intervening air on distant colours and edges
- **Value compression**: the narrowing of value contrast at distance, where lights and darks converge toward a mid-value sky tone
- **Lost edges**: boundaries between shapes that dissolve into soft transitions rather than hard lines

### Puzzle 14: Rayleigh Scattering and the Blue Sky

Why is the sky blue? It is a question that seems simple but has a precise scientific answer that has direct implications for every landscape painter.

**Rayleigh scattering** is the scattering of light by particles (in this case, gas molecules in the atmosphere) that are much smaller than the wavelengths of light. Shorter wavelengths — blue and violet — are scattered far more strongly than longer wavelengths like red and orange. When sunlight enters the atmosphere, blue wavelengths are deflected in all directions, filling the whole sky vault with scattered blue light. Red and orange wavelengths pass through more directly, which is why sunsets — when sunlight travels through maximum atmosphere — go warm and red.

This same scattering is why distant mountains look blue-grey. The light reflected from those mountains must travel through kilometres of atmosphere before reaching your eye. Blue scatter accumulates along that path, and the mountains take on a blue-grey veil. The more atmosphere between observer and object, the more pronounced this blue haze.

For painters this is not a stylistic choice — it is a physical law. Mountains that are 50 kilometres away genuinely are bluer than mountains 5 kilometres away. Knowing the reason allows a painter to apply the effect with confidence and calibrate the intensity correctly based on atmospheric conditions (clear dry air shows less scattering than humid or hazy air).

**Key terms**
- **Rayleigh scattering**: the preferential deflection of short (blue) wavelengths by small atmospheric particles
- **Blue haze**: the accumulation of scattered blue light between the observer and a distant object
- **Colour temperature at distance**: the shift of distant colours toward cooler, bluer hues due to atmospheric scattering

### Puzzle 15: Time-of-Day Colour Temperature

The colour of daylight changes dramatically across the day, and with it the entire colour key of a landscape. Painters who understand these shifts can set a time of day instantly and convincingly; those who ignore them produce lighting that feels timeless in the worst sense — generic and flat.

**Midday light** is the closest to white or slightly blue-white. The sun is overhead, travelling through minimal atmosphere, and shadows are short and hard. Value contrast is high. Colours are relatively neutral and accurate.

**Golden hour** — the hour after sunrise and before sunset — sees the sun at a low angle, passing through far more atmosphere. The blue wavelengths scatter out, leaving the direct sunlight dominated by warm oranges and reds. Shadows are long and cool violet-blue (the complement of the warm direct light). The contrast between warm lit areas and cool shadows creates a richness that painters have sought for centuries.

**Blue hour** — the period just after sunset and before full dark — reverses this. The sky becomes a luminous deep blue as it catches scattered blue light, and everything in shade is bathed in cool blue. Direct warm light disappears. The palette becomes cool, close in value, and often very beautiful.

Understanding these shifts requires coordinating several variables simultaneously: hue temperature, shadow colour, contrast level, and saturation. Changing only one produces a palette that is internally inconsistent — a warm hue temperature with cold shadows and high contrast reads as no time in particular.

**Key terms**
- **Colour temperature**: the position of a light source on the warm (orange) to cool (blue) scale, measured in Kelvin in photography
- **Golden hour**: the period near sunrise or sunset when direct sunlight is warm and low-angled
- **Complementary shadow colour**: the tendency of shadows to lean toward the complement of the dominant light source

## Station 6 — Paint Workbench

### Puzzle 16: Pigment Bias and Vibrant Mixing

Every tube of paint carries a **pigment bias**: a subtle secondary hue leaning beyond its stated colour. A "blue" paint may lean slightly toward green, or slightly toward violet. A "yellow" may lean toward orange, or toward green. These biases are invisible when you look at the pure paint, but they become decisive when you mix.

To mix a vivid green, the temptation is to combine any yellow and any blue. But if the yellow has a red-orange bias (like Cadmium Yellow Deep) and the blue has a red-violet bias (like Ultramarine), then both pigments contain traces of red. Red is green's complement. When those hidden red biases combine, they partially neutralise the green, producing a muted, muddy result even though you mixed a "yellow" and a "blue."

The solution is to understand the **colour bias wheel**: position your yellow on a circle and determine whether its bias leans toward green or orange. Position your blue and determine whether it leans toward green or violet. For a clean, vivid green you want both pigments biased toward each other — a yellow that leans green (like Hansa Yellow) and a blue that also leans green (like Phthalo Blue Green Shade). No hidden complement contamination enters the mix.

This system, sometimes called **split primary mixing**, is one of the most practical tools in a painter's colour vocabulary. It explains why some mixes "sing" and others go dead, and it gives painters a systematic way to choose pigments rather than guessing.

**Key terms**
- **Pigment bias**: the secondary hue direction a paint leans toward beyond its stated colour
- **Complement contamination**: the introduction of a complementary hue through biased pigments, which neutralises chroma
- **Split primary palette**: a mixing system that uses two versions of each primary (one warm-biased, one cool-biased) to enable clean mixing across the full spectrum

### Puzzle 17: Over-mixing and Chromatic Collapse

Every time a complementary pair enters a mix, saturation decreases. A touch of red in a green mix dulls it slightly. More red dulls it further. Eventually the mix reaches a point of **chromatic collapse** — the colour ceases to be a recognisable hue and becomes a muddy, desaturated brown or grey. This is sometimes called **mud**.

Mud is not simply "ugly." It is a permanent state — once you have over-mixed, you cannot easily retrieve the chroma. Adding more vivid paint to a muddied mix tends to just dull the new pigment rather than revive the mixture.

The prevention is procedural: **mix with intention, not improvisation**. Add each pigment deliberately and assess the result before adding more. Understand what you are neutralising and by how much. A small amount of complement can be used to knock a colour back to a naturalistic level — this is intentional neutralising. But each addition is a one-way door, and painters who add paint hoping it will fix things rather than understanding what it will do tend to arrive at mud quickly.

Restraint is also relevant on the palette itself. Pigments that have already been contaminated by other colours from previous mixes will contaminate new mixes in turn. Keeping sections of the palette clean and scraping back mixed areas that are no longer needed is part of colour hygiene.

**Key terms**
- **Chromatic collapse / mud**: the irreversible loss of saturation caused by over-mixing complement pairs
- **Intentional neutralising**: the deliberate use of a complement to reduce chroma in a controlled way
- **Colour hygiene**: the practice of keeping palette areas clean to prevent unintended contamination

### Puzzle 18: Optical Mixing and Pointillism

**Optical mixing** occurs when colours are not physically mixed on the palette but placed as separate small marks side by side. At a sufficient viewing distance, the eye cannot resolve the individual marks and blends them perceptually. The result is a colour that feels different from either of the originals.

The key advantage over physical mixing is luminosity. Physical subtractive mixing always loses energy — combining two pigments absorbs the wavelengths that both carry, reducing the reflected light. Optical mixing bypasses this. Two pure dots of blue and yellow reflecting their full spectra reach the eye separately, and the brain interprets them as green — but a green brighter and more vibrant than any green pigment mixture could produce.

This principle was the basis for **Pointillism**, developed by Georges Seurat and Paul Signac in the 1880s. Seurat worked from scientific colour theory (particularly Chevreul's and Ogden Rood's writings) and applied it systematically — every mark in his paintings is a pure dot of colour, unmixed on the canvas. Viewed close up, his paintings look like a mosaic of dots. From a distance, the optical blending creates colour that shimmers with a quality no direct painting approach achieves.

The technique has limits: very fine detail is lost, and control over exact colour outcome is harder than direct mixing. But the luminosity advantage is real and measurable.

**Key terms**
- **Optical mixing**: perceptual colour blending in the viewer's eye from separate adjacent marks
- **Pointillism**: the Seurat-Signac technique of applying pure colour dots systematically to exploit optical mixing
- **Divisionism**: the broader theoretical framework behind pointillism, concerned with how to divide colour for maximum optical effect

## Station 7 — Design Studio

### Puzzle 19: The 60/30/10 Rule

The **60/30/10 rule** is a proportion guideline used in interior design and graphic design to distribute colour in a way that feels balanced rather than chaotic. The dominant colour covers roughly 60% of the composition, a secondary colour around 30%, and an accent colour about 10%.

The underlying principle is **visual hierarchy**: the eye needs a clear sense of what is dominant and what is subordinate. When three colours compete equally, the eye is uncertain where to settle and the composition feels restless. When one colour clearly owns the space, a second supports it, and a third punctuates it, the composition reads easily.

The ratio also reflects how colour actually behaves in large areas versus small ones. A colour that looks balanced as a small swatch can overwhelm a space when applied at 50% coverage. Saturated accent colours in particular work best in small doses — a little vivid orange in a room or poster goes a long way; a wall of it is exhausting. The 10% accent rule is an intuitive acknowledgement of this perceptual reality.

The rule is not a law — it is a reliable starting point. Understanding why it works (hierarchy, not arbitrary numbers) allows designers and painters to break it intelligently when a specific effect requires it.

**Key terms**
- **Dominant colour**: the hue given the largest proportion of the composition, usually a neutral or moderate tone
- **Accent colour**: the hue given the smallest proportion, usually the most saturated or contrasting
- **Visual hierarchy**: the ordering of elements in a composition so the eye knows what to read first, second, and third

### Puzzle 20: Colour Psychology and Cultural Association

Colours carry meaning before a single word is read. Much of this response is immediate and pre-verbal — rooted in evolutionary associations with light, fire, vegetation, and sky. Red signals biological urgency (blood, fire, ripe fruit). Blue signals stability and depth (sky, water). Yellow signals warmth and energy (sunlight). Green signals growth and safety (vegetation, good foraging terrain).

These associations are real and well-documented in Western design and psychological research. But they are not universal. In many East Asian cultures, white is associated with mourning, not purity. In parts of Latin America, purple carries royal and religious associations different from Northern European conventions. Green can signify jealousy in English but have no such connotation elsewhere.

The practical implication for designers is: know the conventions, and know their cultural limits. Using red to communicate urgency in a Western pharmaceutical context is reliable. Assuming that same convention holds globally is a design failure.

A deeper skill is understanding that **colour alone rarely determines meaning** — context, typography, imagery, and composition all interact. Red in a Valentine's context means love. Red in a financial chart means loss. The colour has not changed; the frame has.

**Key terms**
- **Colour psychology**: the study of how colours affect human perception, emotion, and behaviour
- **Cultural colour association**: the meaning attached to a colour within a specific cultural or historical context
- **Chromatic connotation**: the symbolic or emotional meaning a colour carries in a given context

### Puzzle 21: Chromatic Vibration

**Chromatic vibration** is the optical shimmer that occurs at the boundary between two complementary colours when they are at similar value (lightness). The edge appears to flicker or pulse, as if the colours are fighting for dominance.

The mechanism is this: the eye resolves spatial contrast partly through value (light vs dark) and partly through hue. When two complementary hues meet at identical value, the eye receives strong hue contrast — the two colours are as different as possible on the colour wheel — but no value contrast to anchor the boundary. The visual system cannot easily decide which colour is "figure" and which is "ground." The result is perceptual instability: the edge reads differently depending on how you fixate on it, and it appears to move.

Josef Albers studied this systematically, and artists like Victor Vasarely and Bridget Riley built entire careers on the phenomenon, becoming the founders of the **Op Art** movement in the 1960s. Their paintings use carefully controlled value equality combined with complementary or near-complementary hues to produce grids, stripes, and curves that appear to pulsate, advance, or recede even on a flat surface.

The effect is maximised at full saturation — muted or grey-shifted complements reduce the hue conflict and stabilise the edge. It also depends critically on value equality: even a small value difference between the two colours is enough for the eye to anchor the boundary and kill the vibration.

**Key terms**
- **Chromatic vibration**: optical shimmering at the boundary of complementary, equal-value colours
- **Figure-ground ambiguity**: the inability of the visual system to clearly assign figure and background roles, causing perceptual instability
- **Op Art**: a movement using precise optical and colour relationships to produce kinetic visual effects on flat surfaces
