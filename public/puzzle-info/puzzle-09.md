### Puzzle 9: Colour and Emotional Tone

Colour combinations communicate mood before the viewer reads a single word or consciously processes any image. This is partly cultural convention, partly hardwired response to light and temperature.

**Warm hues** — reds, oranges, yellows — activate and energise. They are associated with fire, sunlight, and physical warmth, and they tend to advance toward the viewer optically. **Cool hues** — blues, blue-greens, violets — recede and calm. They are associated with sky, water, and shadow.

But hue temperature alone does not determine mood. **Value** and **saturation** contribute equally. High saturation + high value contrast = excitement and urgency (think carnival posters). Low saturation + close values = introspection and calm (think mist, early morning fog). Dark values + desaturated hues = dread or solemnity (think Gothic interiors, horror film palettes). Understanding this means a "calm" palette is not simply blue — it requires low-contrast values, moderate saturation, and likely cool hues working together.

A further nuance: creative subversion of expectations carries meaning too. A horror film that uses pastel pinks and yellows is disturbing *because* those colours are "supposed" to be safe. The mismatch between expectation and context creates unease. Competent colour storytelling requires knowing the convention in order to break it intentionally.

**Key terms**
- **Colour temperature**: the warm (orange-red) to cool (blue-violet) axis of the colour wheel
- **Simultaneous contrast**: how surrounding colours alter the perceived temperature and mood of a focal colour
- **Chromatic key**: the overall emotional register of a palette, determined by hue, value, and saturation together
