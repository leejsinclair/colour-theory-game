# Puzzle 23 — Colour Constancy

Colour is not fixed at the eye. The same surface can send warmer light under tungsten bulbs and cooler light under daylight, yet we often still perceive a stable material colour.

That stability is called **colour constancy**. Your brain compares the object with its surroundings, estimates the light source, and partially discounts the cast.

In this puzzle you will compare the same object under warm, cool, and neutral light. The goal is not to pick the colour that matches one panel best, but the swatch that most plausibly explains **all three panels at once**.

## How To Win

- Study the object in all three lighting panels.
- Compare how the apparent colour shifts between warm and cool light.
- Choose the swatch that represents the underlying surface colour across the full set.

## Why This Fails

- Picking a warm- or cool-shifted swatch means you matched the lighting cast instead of the surface.
- Picking a different hue means the object would not transform into the shown appearances under the three lights.

## Quiz

1. Why can a white shirt look orange under candlelight yet still be perceived as white?
   - Because the brain compensates for the warm light source.

2. What is the best strategy for solving colour constancy problems?
   - Choose the option that remains plausible under every lighting condition, not just one.
